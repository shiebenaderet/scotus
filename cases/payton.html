Background Information

Understanding the facts and constitutional questions

What Happened?

In 1970, the police in New York City suspected Theodore Payton of murder. They had strong evidence against him, including witness testimony and physical evidence. However, instead of going to a judge to get an arrest warrant, police officers went directly to Payton's home.

When Payton was not home, the police entered his house and searched it without permission. They found evidence of the murder in plain view. Later, when Payton was arrested without a warrant, he was convicted and sentenced to life in prison.

The Big Question

Can police enter a suspect's home to make an arrest without a warrant? Does the Fourth Amendment require warrants for home arrests?

What You Need to Know

The Fourth Amendment protects people's homes from "unreasonable searches and seizures." Usually, this means that police must have a warrant signed by a judge before entering a private home. But can the requirement for a warrant be waived when police have probable cause to make an arrest?

Important Facts

  • Payton was suspected of murder
  • Police had probable cause but no warrant
  • Police entered Payton's home without permission
  • Evidence was found in plain view
  • Payton was convicted based on this evidence

Vocabulary

Probable Cause: Reasonable belief that a crime has been committed
Warrant: Legal document signed by a judge authorizing search or arrest
Exigent Circumstances: Emergency situations allowing warrantless entry

What Happened?

A long time ago, police in New York thought a man named Theodore Payton hurt someone. They had good reasons to think this. But they did not do one important thing first.

Police are usually supposed to get permission from a judge before going into someone's house. This permission is called a warrant. But the police did not get a warrant.

Instead, they went right to Payton's house and went inside without asking. They looked around and found things. Later, Payton was sent to prison based on what they found.

The Big Question

Can police go into someone's house without getting permission from a judge first?

Important Facts

  • Police thought Payton hurt someone
  • Police had good reasons, but no permission
  • Police went into his house anyway
  • They found things there

Vocabulary

Warrant: Permission from a judge
Police: People who catch bad guys
Permission: When someone says you can do something

The Arguments

Legal reasoning from both sides

Payton's Arguments

  • The Fourth Amendment protects people's homes from unreasonable searches and seizures
  • The Supreme Court has always required warrants before police can search inside homes
  • If police can arrest people in homes without warrants, it would make the warrant requirement meaningless
  • A person's home deserves the strongest protection under the Fourth Amendment
  • Getting a warrant would not create a big burden - police already need probable cause
  • The Constitution says homes are special places
  • Police should get permission before going into someone's house
  • Without permission, people's homes are not safe

New York's Arguments

  • Police have historically been allowed to arrest suspects in public places with probable cause
  • Criminals might destroy evidence or escape if police have to wait for a warrant
  • Prior to this case, many states allowed warrantless home arrests
  • Common law historically allowed arrests in homes without warrants
  • The exigent circumstances exception allows warrantless entry in emergencies
  • Police need to catch bad guys quickly
  • Waiting for permission takes too long
  • Bad guys might run away or hide things
